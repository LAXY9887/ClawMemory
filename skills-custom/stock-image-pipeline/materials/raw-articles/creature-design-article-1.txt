The Obsidian Glitch: Echoes from the Synthetic Abyss
The air within Sector 9 did not drift; it vibrated. Deep beneath the crust of the sleeping city, the silence was not an absence of sound, but a heavy, pressurized weight held back only by thick layers of reinforced crystalline shielding. Beyond those transparent barriers, the air was a thick, swirling haze of incandescent violet vapor, a byproduct of the entity’s constant metabolic friction.

We called it the Coded Revenant, though the official logs simply listed it as a "Level 5 Kinetic Irregularity."

At the heart of the containment vault, the creature hovered—if one could call such a jagged, defiant existence "hovering." It was a nightmare of fractured geometry and visceral electricity. Its primary form resembled a massive, elongated predator, but its surface was never static. Instead of skin or scales, its body was composed of shimmering, overlapping shards of obsidian glass that pulsed with a rhythmic, cobalt luminescence. Between these dark plates, one could glimpse the terrifying truth of its anatomy: a network of raw, exposed nervous filaments that glowed with the intensity of an overloaded circuit.

As it moved, the beast didn't slice through space; it seemed to tear through it. Every twitch of its segmented, carbon-fiber-like limbs left behind a trail of distorted reality, as if the very air was struggling to render its presence. Specks of golden light—looking more like floating dead pixels than dust—swirled around its tail, which whipped through the containment field with a sound like tearing metal.

"We huddle around our flickering screens, much like our ancestors did around their fires," Director Vane whispered, staring through the observation pane. "We think we have mapped the world, but we have only built better cages for the things that shouldn't exist."

Suddenly, the entity turned. It didn't have eyes in any biological sense. Instead, its "face" was a cluster of flickering, rectangular sensors that projected a harsh, emerald beam across the room. The light hit the observation glass, scanning the researchers with a cold, predatory intelligence. Where the light touched the walls, ancient, glowing symbols—reminiscent of both forgotten languages and high-level encryption—began to burn into the concrete.

The alarm system didn't just ring; it screamed in a harmonized digital screech. A warning signal of intense crimson flooded the corridor as the creature pressed a limb against the barrier. The contact point erupted in a spray of phosphorescent sparks. The glass didn't shatter—it began to dissolve into liquid light, dripping toward the floor like molten mercury.

A low-frequency hum began to rattle the bones of everyone in the room. The entity was projecting its own reality, a glitching landscape where the laws of physics were merely suggestions. Around it, the floor tiles began to rise and spin in perfectly symmetrical patterns, floating in a sea of static-filled shadows.

The Revenant let out a sound—not a roar, but a harmonic collision of white noise and cathedral bells. It was the sound of a god born from a server farm, a ghost trapped in a machine of flesh and wire. As the final layer of the containment field flickered and died, the creature expanded, its shards unfolding like the wings of a fallen angel made of dark chrome and starlight.

For a moment, there was a blinding flash of spectral white, and then, the vault was empty. The only thing remaining was a single, translucent feather made of pure data, drifting slowly toward the floor, and the faint, lingering smell of ozone and old parchment.
